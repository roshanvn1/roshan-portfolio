SLAM Obstacle Avoiding Robot

During my vacation I wanted to learn and gain experience in practical project implementation, so I built a DIY LiDAR-based SLAM and obstacle-avoiding robot as a hobby project. This took more time than my two-month vacation as I was doing everything alone, debugging and fixing constant problems—all under a ₹2000 budget.

Hardware Structure

  • Bottom layer: DC motors, motor driver, rechargeable batteries, buck converter
  • Middle layer: ESP32 microcontroller
  • Top layer: IMU, servo motor with a VL53L0X LiDAR mounted inside a lightweight bottle cap (a cheap, strong DIY enclosure)

Features & Operations

  • Scans the surroundings using a rotating LiDAR and sends readings to a Flask-based website.
  • The website processes the data through my laptop and displays a live map.
  • Robot moves a fixed distance, rescans, and sends updates wirelessly.
  • If an obstacle is detected, it rescans and precisely turns toward the direction with more free space.
  • Full control (start/stop/reset/stop generating map/clear points) available through the web interface.

Limitations Managed

  • Needs 1 minute of IMU calibration for accuracy at the beginning.
  • ESP32 gets overloaded if it runs too long.
  • Queue buildup: Robot sends data faster than the web processing speed, which I managed by slowing down the robot and optimizing data flow.

Skills Gained

Sensor fusion, Real-time data processing, Flask-based IoT communication, Embedded programming on ESP32, Mapping algorithms, Low-cost hardware design.

Click for more details

Analog Obstacle Avoiding Robot

As part of my S4 Analog Circuits-2 course, I designed and built a collision-avoiding robot. The entire circuit design, logic, and assembly were done by me from scratch without the use of microcontrollers.

Hardware & Control

The robot uses two gear motors and a freewheel for movement, controlled by LM358 (op-amp as comparator), an L293D motor driver, a 555 timer IC, and three pairs of IR LEDs and receivers for obstacle detection.

To avoid jerks caused by motor current fluctuations, power is separated: a 7805 regulator powers the control section from a 9V supply, while the motors are driven by an 18650 battery through the L293D driver. The motor speed can be adjusted precisely using the 555 timer.

Binary Logic Design

For obstacle detection, I developed a simple but powerful binary logic using the three IR sensor pairs (1 = object detected, 0 = no object, X = either).
MSB: Front sensor | Middle bit: Left sensor | LSB: Right sensor

  • 100 - Robot stops
  • X10 - Robot turns strictly right until clear
  • X01 - Robot turns strictly left until clear

Development Journey

The final design came after intensive brainstorming and testing. I tried three different op-amps before finding that the LM358 worked best as a comparator here. For motor control, I experimented with MOSFETs and transistors, but they caused heating and wasted power, prompting a switch to the L293D. Because the system is fully analog, the response is actually faster and more accurate compared to traditional microcontroller setups!

Custom IR Transmitter & Receiver

As a team, we built a fully functional infrared communication system from scratch without relying on pre-built transceiver modules.

The Transmitter

  • Built an astable multivibrator utilizing two 2N2222 transistors to generate high-frequency square wave signals.
  • One output connects to a visible LED to indicate the current frequency visually, while the other connects to an IR LED via a voltage divider to provide it with balanced power.
  • Used a DPDT switch to toggle between two distinct frequencies dynamically using 30kΩ and 100kΩ resistors.

The Receiver

  • Utilized a BS0038 IR receiver connected to a 2N2222 transistor and an LED to detect and amplify the incoming infrared signal.

Outcomes

Throughout this process, we deeply learned the intricate design and behavior of an astable multivibrator for signal generation, and enhanced our practical circuit design skills through iterative problem-solving.

Custom DIY Driller

Back in May 2021, as part of my electronics hobby, I realized I didn’t have a PCB driller on hand, so I decided to make one myself using an RS-775 DC motor (24V). I repurposed some extra leftover PVC pipe from the construction of my home for the casing. This project allowed me to channel my creativity and refine my practical skills.

System Design

  • Power Source: 24V 2.5A adapter.
  • Switching Mechanism: Equipped with a main power switch, a DPDT switch to toggle between speed-controlled operation (via voltage regulation) and max power, and a second DPDT switch to selectively reverse rotation (clockwise/anti-clockwise).
  • Speed Controller: Built using a 2SC5200 transistor and a 10k potentiometer for precision control.
  • Compact Enclosure: The circuit was specifically built to fit neatly within the PVC pipe handle, ensuring every component has sufficient space to function safely without crowding.

What started as a solution to a simple need turned into a fun and rewarding exercise in electrical layout, with the added bonus of repurposing materials directly from home construction.
